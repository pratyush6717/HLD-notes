Two different problems — two different tools. Rate Limiter controls how many requests come IN from clients. Bulkhead controls how many threads go OUT to downstream services. Confusing them = wrong protection in wrong place.
Problem 1 — Client overload
  • Client sends 1000 req/sec
  • System can handle 100 req/sec
  • Server crashes from sheer volume
  • Solution: Rate Limiter at the gate
Problem 2 — Downstream slow
  • Order Service calls Payment
  • Payment responds in 5 seconds
  • Threads pile up waiting for Payment
  • Fast APIs also start failing
Mental model — super clear:

Rate Limiter → OUTSIDE protection (from clients)
Bulkhead → INSIDE protection (between services/resources)

Traffic control ≠ Resource control

Rate Limiter = Gatekeeper. Sits at the edge of your service. Controls how many requests clients can send per time window. Doesn't care about concurrency — just rate.
Rate limiter flow
Client (1000 req/sec) ↓ Rate Limiter — max 100 req/sec ↓ 100 allowed · 900 rejected (HTTP 429) Order Service (stable, never overloaded)
Important nuance
Rate limiter allows N requests per window — whether they come all at once or spread out. It doesn't care if 10 hit simultaneously.
Allow 10/sec:
✔ 10 arriving at t=0 (all at once)
✔ 1 per 100ms (spread out)
✗ 11th request → rejected

No concurrency control — all 10 could be processed simultaneously.
Mall entry gate analogy: "100 people per minute allowed." They can all rush in at once at the start of the minute — gate doesn't regulate them once inside. Rate Limiter is the gate, not a crowd controller inside the mall.
Bulkhead = Ship compartments. Ships have watertight compartments — one floods, others stay dry. Bulkhead isolates thread pools so one slow service can't steal threads meant for another.
Simple bulkhead
Order Service calls Product ↓ Product handles only 3 concurrent Bulkhead — max 3 concurrent threads ↓
Req1 ✔ Req2 ✔ Req3 ✔ Req4 ✗ wait/reject
↓ Product Service (never overwhelmed)
Key difference from Rate Limiter
Bulkhead controls how many requests are simultaneously active — not how many came per second.
Bulkhead = 3 concurrent max:
✗ All 3 threads busy at same time → 4th rejected
✔ As soon as one finishes → 4th enters

Doesn't care if 100 arrive in 1 second — only 3 active at any moment.
Restaurant kitchen analogy: Veg section, non-veg section, dessert section — all separate. Non-veg gets overwhelmed → veg chef still working calmly. Sections = bulkheads. Isolation prevents one busy section from killing the whole kitchen.
Most important real use case. Order Service has 2 APIs: API1 calls Product (fast), API2 calls Payment (slow). Payment spike → all 10 threads blocked → API1 also starts failing. Thread Pool Bulkhead fixes this.

Noisy neighbor definition: One component consumes all shared resources — starving others. Thread Pool Bulkhead = give each component its own dedicated pool. Noisy neighbor can only hurt itself.
Rate Limiter: Protects the system from excessive client traffic by limiting requests per time window.

Bulkhead: Isolates resources so failure or slowness in one part does not impact others.
Decision guide — which to use?
ScenarioUseWhy
Client spamming your APIRate LimiterControl incoming traffic rate — reject excess
